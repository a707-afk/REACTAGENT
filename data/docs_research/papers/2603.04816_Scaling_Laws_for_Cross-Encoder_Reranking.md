###### Abstract.


Scaling laws have been observed across a wide range of tasks, such as natural language generation and dense retrieval, where performance follows predictable patterns as model size, data, and compute grow. However, these scaling laws are insufficient for understanding the scaling behavior of multi-stage retrieval systems, which typically include a reranking stage. In large-scale multi-stage retrieval systems, reranking is the final and most influential step before presenting a ranked list of items to the end user. In this work, we present the first systematic study of scaling laws for rerankers by analyzing performance across model sizes and data budgets for three popular paradigms: pointwise, pairwise, and listwise reranking. Using a detailed case study with cross-encoder rerankers, we demonstrate that performance follows a predictable power law. This regularity allows us to accurately forecast the performance of larger models for some metrics more than others using smaller-scale experiments, offering a robust methodology for saving significant computational resources. For example, we accurately estimate the NDCG of a 1B-parameter model by training and evaluating only smaller models (up to 400M parameters), in both in-domain as well as out-of-domain settings. Our results establish scaling principles for reranking and provide actionable insights for building industrial-grade retrieval systems. We will release the code and evaluation protocols used for training, evaluation, and large-scale performance forecasting here111https://github.com/rahulseetharaman/cross-encoder-scaling.

##
1. Introduction


Modern search engines often follow multi-stage retrieval pipelines (X Engineering Blog, 2023; Nagar et al., 2025; Johnson, 2025; Bing Image Search Relevance Team, 2018; Vorotilov and Shugaepov, 2023). Such pipelines typically employ an efficient retrieval model, such as BM25 (Robertson et al., 1995), to retrieve a relatively small number of candidates from a very large corpus, followed by one or more reranking stages in which the candidates from the last stage are reranked (Nogueira and Cho, 2019; Wang et al., 2011; Hu et al., 2019; Hofstätter et al., 2021b). While the objective in the early stage retrievers is to maximize recall, the objectives in later stages of the funnel is to maximize ranking quality, which is often measured by metrics like NDCG (normalized discounted cumulative gain).


While the principles of scaling are well-established for natural language generation (Kaplan et al., 2020) and, more recently, for dense retrieval (Fang et al., 2024) (Zeng and others, 2025) and embedding scaling (Killingback et al., 2026), a similar characterization for reranking is underexplored. Reranking differs from first-stage retrieval in ways that make scaling behavior non-obvious: rerankers operate on a candidate set induced by a retriever (so the decision space is conditional on upstream retrieval), they are trained with heterogeneous learning-to-rank objectives (pointwise/pairwise/ listwise), and they are evaluated using discontinuous top-k metrics such as NDCG. As a result, scaling laws established for language modeling or dense retrieval do not directly imply predictable scaling for rerankers. Besides, reranking is the final, high-precision stage of a retrieval pipeline, where a computationally intensive model refines a small set of candidates. The high cost of training large-scale reranking models makes them a prime candidate for scaling analysis.


Our goal is to forecast the downstream performance of large rerankers using only smaller-scale training runs, so that we can plan expensive training at 1B+ scale with fewer full-size experiments. We frame this through four research questions (RQs) to be addressed independently for each paradigm (pointwise, pairwise and listwise).


•


RQ1 (Model Scaling): Can we predict the performance of large reranking models from smaller reranking models trained on the same data?


•


RQ2 (Data Scaling): With model size held fixed, how does ranking quality scale as a function of training data exposure (i.e., number of training examples consumed / training progress), and can we forecast later-stage performance from earlier checkpoints?


•


RQ3 (Compute Scaling): When scaling both model capacity and training data exposure together, can a simple joint scaling law accurately predict downstream ranking quality across the grid of
(model size, data exposure)?


•


RQ4 (Objective sensitivity): Do scaling laws differ across pointwise, pairwise, and listwise fine-tuning objectives?


In that regard, we present the first systematic study of scaling laws for rerankers. To reliably predict the performance of a large reranker by observing the behavior of smaller rerankers, we examine how reranking performance scales across three primary learning-to-rank paradigms (pointwise, pairwise, and listwise) as model size and labeled data grow.


Through comprehensive experimentation on a set of cross encoder (Devlin et al., 2019; Nogueira and Cho, 2019) models of varying sizes, finetuned on 100K MSMARCO queries, we demonstrate ranking metrics like NDCG follow smooth, predictable power laws and scale with model and dataset size. Crucially, these laws exhibit strong predictive power, enabling accurate forecasts of large-model performance from small-scale experiments. To our knowledge, this is the first study to systematically characterize scaling laws for neural rerankers across pointwise, pairwise, and listwise objectives. From our extensive experiments, we have the following key observations – 1) We observe predictable power law scaling across model, data and joint scaling axes for MSMARCO-dev and the TREC DL evaluation sets (Craswell et al., 2020, 2025, 2021, 2022; Mackie et al., 2021) 2) It is possible to accurately forecast a downstream metric (NDCG@10) for the 1B model by training on model sizes up to 400M.


This paper is organized as follows: In Section 2, we first discuss related work in reranking and scaling laws. In Sections 3 and 4, we introduce the three reranking paradigms and our scaling framework. In Section 5, we describe our experiments, and then discuss the results in Section 6.


##
2. Background


##### Multi-Stage Retrieval and Reranking.


Modern search systems are commonly organized as multi-stage pipelines, where an efficient first-stage retriever prioritizes recall by selecting a small candidate set, and a later-stage reranker prioritizes precision by reordering those candidates with a more expressive model. Reranking operates on a conditional decision set induced by the upstream retriever, and is evaluated with top- `k` ranking metrics (e.g., NDCG@10) that are discontinuous and sensitive to local ordering. These properties make it unclear whether scaling trends established for pretraining or first-stage retrieval transfer to rerankers in a predictable way.


##### Scaling Laws and Forecasting in Machine Learning.


Scaling laws characterize how performance changes as we increase model capacity, data, and compute. Early work on learning curves in neural networks identified regularities in generalization with scale (Cortes et al., 1993), and subsequent studies showed that language model loss often follows smooth power-law trends with respect to parameters, data, and compute (Kaplan et al., 2020), enabling performance forecasting and motivating compute-optimal training strategies such as Chinchilla (Hoffmann et al., 2022).
Scaling analyses have since been extended across modalities, including vision (Zhai et al., 2022), multimodal learning (Aghajanyan et al., 2023), and regimes that examine effectively unbounded compute (Kim et al., 2025). However, forecasting downstream task metrics can be substantially less reliable than forecasting training loss, prompting recent work on when scaling-based prediction succeeds and how to improve its robustness (Xu et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2025). This reliability gap is especially relevant for retrieval, where practitioners care about ranking metrics rather than perplexity-style objectives.


##### Scaling Laws in Retrieval.


The retrieval community has recently begun to establish systematic scaling behavior for first-stage retrieval components.
Prior work shows consistent gains from scaling retrieval-augmented datastores (Shao and others, 2024), and analyzes how sparse and dense retrieval mechanisms emerge and scale in decoder-only models, with scaling behavior depending on training objectives and compute budgets (Zeng and others, 2025).
Complementary work studies generative retrieval and introduces objectives that enable continuous scaling measurement, revealing log-linear and power-law trends across training and inference compute (Cai and others, 2025).
Collectively, these results indicate that retrieval quality can exhibit predictable behavior along axes of model capacity, data, and compute.
However, these studies primarily focus on retrieval as approximate candidate selection over a corpus, rather than fine-grained reranking over a retriever-induced candidate set.


##### Reranking Objectives and the Missing Scaling Picture.


Rerankers typically evaluate a small set of retrieved candidates using richer interaction models (often cross-encoders), and are trained using diverse learning-to-rank objectives.
Classical learning-to-rank formulations distinguish pointwise, pairwise, and listwise paradigms (Liu, 2009), with representative approaches such as RankNet (Burges et al., 2005a), RankSVM (Joachims, 2002), and ListNet (Cao et al., 2007).
Neural reranking has since evolved substantially with pretrained language models and cross-encoder architectures (Nogueira and Cho, 2019; Ni et al., 2021; MacAvaney et al., 2020), and many works study efficiency techniques such as distillation and architectural approximations to reduce inference cost (Hofstätter et al., 2021a; Nogueira et al., 2020).
Despite these advances, we lack a principled understanding of whether reranker ranking quality scales predictably with model size and training exposure, and whether scaling behavior depends on the underlying objective (pointwise/pairwise/listwise).


Our paper targets this gap: we study scaling laws for rerankers, where the evaluation target is a downstream top- `k` ranking metric (NDCG@10) and the decision set is conditioned on a first-stage retriever.
We ask whether we can forecast large-model reranking performance from smaller-scale training runs, along model, data-exposure, and joint scaling axes, and whether the fitted trends differ across learning-to-rank paradigms.
Because discontinuous ranking metrics can yield noisier curves, we also analyze contrastive entropy as a secondary continuous diagnostic (similar in spirit to proxy losses used in prior scaling work), while keeping NDCG@10 as the primary forecasting target.


##
3. Reranking Paradigms


Given a query `q` and candidate set `\mathcal{C}(q)=\{d_{1},\dots,d_{K}\}` , a reranker is a scoring function `f_{\theta}(q,d_{i})\mapsto s_{i}\in\mathbb{R}` that induces a ranking `\pi(q)=\operatorname{argsort}_{i}s_{i}` . We focus on three paradigms, and report all evaluations per paradigm:


•


Pointwise. Each example is a tuple `(q,d,y)` . The model predicts a per-document relevance label by minimizing a regression or cross-entropy loss over individual scores `f_{\theta}(q,d_{i})` . In our experiments, we use Binary Cross Entropy Loss for the pointwise setting.


•


Pairwise. Each example is a triplet `(q,d^{+},d^{-})` . The model is trained so that `f_{\theta}(q,d^{+})>f_{\theta}(q,d^{-})` via a pairwise margin loss. We leverage RankNet loss (Burges et al., 2005b) for the pairwise setting.


•


Listwise. Each example is a query with a full document list and labels `(q,\mathbf{d},\mathbf{y})` . The model is trained with a permutation-aware, list-level objective. We leverage ListNet loss (Xia et al., 2008) for the listwise setting.

4. A Framework for Reranker Scaling Laws


In this section, we present our framework for systematically analyzing reranker scaling laws. We begin by training three sets of smaller-scale reranking models, varying both the amount of training data and model size across the three reranking paradigms. We then extrapolate from the small-scale results to predict the performance of a larger model, and evaluate the RMSE between the actual performance and the extrapolated estimates.

Unlike dense retrieval, where random negatives probe separability in a global corpus space, reranking operates on a candidate set induced by a first-stage retriever. We use BM25 as our first-stage retriever, and hence compute CE over the BM25 top-100 candidates (sampling 64 negatives), reflecting the reranker’s actual decision set. To reduce sensitivity to score calibration across training paradigms, we normalize scores. We report both CE and NDCG: CE provides a smoother signal for scaling analysis, while NDCG measures downstream ranking quality.


While NDCG@10 is our primary downstream metric, it is a discontinuous top-k measure and can introduce noise in curve fitting. We therefore also report contrastive entropy (CE) as a continuous diagnostic signal, analogous to perplexity-style proxies used in other scaling-law settings. Importantly, our goal is not to replace downstream evaluation: we fit and forecast scaling laws directly on NDCG@10. We also fit scaling laws over CE to help interpret training dynamics (e.g., margins and calibration) when it is stable.


To measure how successful a fit is, previous approaches have focused on reporting the goodness of fit using metrics like the coefficient of determination, which don’t directly shed light on performance at unknown compute. Instead, we measure the error (RMSE) at a held-out compute by not using the data used for fitting the curve. This helps directly gauge the downstream effectiveness of a scaling fit.

##
5. Experiments


We use the Ettin cross-encoder series (Weller et al., 2025) of models at six different sizes for our experiments – 17M, 32M, 68M, 150M, 400M and 1B. We finetune each one of them on 100k queries from the MS MARCO passage ranking dataset. For the pointwise paradigm, we use a batch size of 128, a learning rate of 2e-5, and fine-tune for 1 epoch. For listwise and pairwise paradigms, each batch consists of 16 queries, 1 positive document, and 10 anchor negatives, making the effective batch size to be 160. For all our expriments, we employ the same learning rates and finetune our models for 1 epoch.


During the evaluation process, we leverage BM25 as a first stage retriever and the MSMARCO-dev set as our evaluation dataset. We take top 100 passages retrieved by BM25 and leverage our above trained models to rerank them, and compute our evaluation metrics (NDCG@10, contrastive entropy). We also present evaluations with TREC DL ’19-’23 and HARD.


##### Model Scaling


: To understand model scaling behaviour, the last checkpoint for every model size is used to plot the scaling curves.


##### Data Scaling


: We operationalize data scaling as training data exposure: as training progresses through one epoch, checkpoints correspond to an increasing number of consumed training examples, while the underlying dataset remains fixed. We take the 150M models (for pairwise, pointwise, and listwise) and fit power law functions to model observed NDCG as a function of training step and predict the observed NDCG for the last 5 steps.


##### Joint Scaling

6.2. Scaling laws on Contrastive Entropy (CE)


####
6.2.1. Model Scaling for CE


In addition to NDCG@10, we also fit scaling functions for data, model and joint scaling on Contrastive Entropy (CE). We fit the same power law formulations as used for NDCG.


####
6.2.2. Data Scaling for CE


Similar to the NDCG setting, we fit power laws on the 150M model’s checkpoints for pointwise, pairwise and listwise objectives and report the results in Figure 1(e) and Table 2. It is noted that while NDCG followed very predictable power law scaling, contrastive entropy trends vary across paradigms - for instance, for the pairwise setting the data scaling axis doesn’t follow a clear decrease in CE with increasing step size. This illustrates a key limitation of using contrastive entropy in a reranker scaling context - effect of model calibration and normalization can cause fluctuations in contrastive entropy, since it is a metric which is sensitive to the actual scores that is produced by a reranker. A downstream metric like NDCG relies solely on the relative ordering and follows predictable scaling trends even as contrastive entropy fluctuates. Besides, the curves illustrate that the best fit for contrastive entropy may also vary from paradigm to paradigm and power law is not necessarily the best fit for every objective.

##
9. Conclusion


We present the first systematic framework for analyzing scaling laws in reranking. Through comprehensive experiments across pointwise, pairwise, and listwise reranking, we demonstrate that reranker performance follows predictable power laws with model and data scale with traditional metrics like NDCG. Such scaling behaviour enables accurate forecasting of downstream ranking quality (e.g., NDCG@10) in the large-model regime using only smaller-scale training runs, providing a practical approach for planning expensive reranker training.


We also find that scaling behavior (fit quality and apparent exponents) differs across pointwise, pairwise, and listwise objectives, suggesting that a single universal fit is unlikely to capture all paradigms equally well.


Similarly, while metrics like MAP simialrly show reliable scaling behavior, MRR does not show predictable scaling on all evaluation datasets.


Finally, while contrastive entropy is a useful continuous proxy in dense-retrieval scaling analyses, it is less reliable for reranker forecasting in our setting due to sensitivity to score calibration and normalization: ranking improvements can occur even when score margins fluctuate. Overall, our results establish actionable scaling principles for reranking: practitioners can use small-scale sweeps to predict large-scale NDCG outcomes, allocate compute more efficiently, and choose objectives with awareness of their differing scaling behavior. Our work entails interesting future studies including extending these analyses to other candidate generation methods beyond BM25, broader out-of-domain benchmarks, alternative reranker architectures (including late interaction and generative rerankers), and studying how candidate set size and retrieval quality interact with reranker scaling.
